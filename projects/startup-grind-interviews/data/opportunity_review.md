### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Marc Barros, Co-founder Moment (interviewed 2015-10-06)

Barros identifies his core failure as first-time CEO of Contour as never making the mental transition from "founder" to "CEO" — nobody explained to him that these require opposite skill sets. Her framing of how to use that feedback is itself a nuance: you don't adopt criticism wholesale, you extract what's relevant and let it sharpen your own perspective and pitch — treating a wide net of blunt conversations as a low-cost way to pressure-test an idea before it's expensive to change.

To build a Moment-style "content to commerce" engine similar to what made GoPro dominant, Barros structured the team so roughly half of it works on product and the other half is dedicated specifically to content that inspires people and converts into sales (rather than treating content/marketing as a bolt-on function). Describing how his first company grew from zero to $32M in sales, Parker credits a deliberate growth hack: rather than spreading effort evenly, they picked Microsoft's weakest-performing regional sales office (ranked 8th of 9 on a metric Microsoft's regions were tracked on) and concentrated investment there, taking it from 8th to 2nd place. The transferable insight: concentrating resources on making one underperforming segment into a standout reference case can generate organic pull from every other segment, rather than trying to win broadly and thinly from the start.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Howard Behar, Former President Starbucks (interviewed 2018-05-10)

That result contradicted the conventional retail instinct to avoid clustering stores so close together for fear of cannibalization, and it became the basis for Starbucks's later strategy of opening multiple stores close together in dense urban corridors.

It's a concrete counter-example to the instinct that a splashy press hit is the highest-leverage marketing move for a consumer product.

Godreau describes only two engagement models with corporate strategic partners that actually work, and warns that the middle ground doesn't: either (1) bring a partner in very early as an informal co-development collaborator -- not a cap-table investor -- so their insight shapes the product before it's built, or (2) bring them in late, only after you've already proven your value proposition and have a working distribution channel, at which point co-op marketing dollars and real partnership terms become available. Parker calls out a common but counterproductive founder habit: telling existing customers about a cool feature that's coming soon. ## Hiring & People Ops

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Marc Barros, Co-founder Moment (interviewed 2015-10-06)

Rather than posting job listings and running formal interviews, Moment never hires anyone "off the street." Instead, every hire starts as a small, paid side project (a few hours or a day a week) before any full-time offer is discussed. Barros argues this reveals more in a single day of actual collaboration than any interview process could — you learn instantly whether someone communicates well and is good at the work — and it also makes it much easier and less awkward to end things if it isn't working out, compared to firing someone you've already onboarded full time.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Jason Stoffer, Partner Maveron (interviewed 2015-12-07)

Stoffer offers a specific staffing rule of thumb for founders: everyone at the company should personally interview every new hire until headcount reaches 100 employees. The implicit reasoning is that founder involvement in hiring is what preserves culture and quality bar during the highest-leverage growth phase, and 100 is the concrete threshold past which that becomes structurally impossible.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dave Parker, CEO CodeFellows (interviewed 2017-01-12)

Parker identifies hiring a salesperson too early — before the product is actually ready to ship — as one of the worst early hiring mistakes a startup can make. The mechanism: a salesperson's value comes from hunting and closing deals, but if there's nothing to sell yet, they get stuck in strategy meetings and whiteboarding sessions for 90 days. By the time there is a product, that person has been reprogrammed by the role into a strategist rather than a closer, and doesn't want to go back to grinding sales calls. The lesson generalizes beyond sales: hiring any specialist role before there's real work for it to do can permanently change what that person is good at and willing to do.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dave Parker, CEO CodeFellows (interviewed 2017-01-12)

Parker's method for firing fast without being reckless is a structured 30/60/90-day review, where each stage answers a different specific question rather than being one long informal probation. At 30 days, the question is purely cultural fit. At 60 days, it's whether the person can actually perform the mechanics of the role (e.g., can a developer commit code at a reasonable pace, can a salesperson start converting). At 90 days, it's a formal go/no-go: if all the signals aren't firing together by then, the person is let go rather than given another 90-day extension, which he's seen turn into a full year of denial about a bad fit.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Adrian Hanauer, Owner Seattle Sounders FC (interviewed 2016-04-26)

Hanauer identifies a specific blind spot in the Sounders' own youth academy pipeline: some of the most skillful young players, particularly kids from Central American, South American, and Mexican immigrant families, are not part of the organized club soccer system that academies typically scout from. This runs against the assumption that a well-structured academy (with age groups from under-12 through under-18) will naturally surface the best local talent — in practice, talent identification is skewed toward kids whose families are already inside the pay-to-play club system, and the organization openly admits this is an area where it needs to do better.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Liz Pearce, CEO LiquidPlanner (interviewed 2014-06-27)

LiquidPlanner's engineering team reached 5 out of 11 engineers (45%) female, which Pearce attributes directly to sponsoring ADA Developers Academy, a coding bootcamp specifically for women changing careers from non-technical backgrounds like teaching or linguistics, and bringing on an ADA apprentice — over initial internal pushback from her own CTO, whose objection was that the company already had a good pipeline of University of Washington computer-science interns. The actionable point: adding a career-changer bootcamp partnership to a hiring funnel that already looks adequate can still measurably shift team composition within a few hiring cycles.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Bill Bryant, Partner DFJ Ventures (interviewed 2016-02-04)

Bryant says the best CEOs he works with spend roughly 20-25% of their time on passive talent cultivation -- not active recruiting for open roles, but ongoing relationship-building with people who might become relevant hires 18 months in the future -- and that this holds true at any company size up until roughly 100 employees, after which a dedicated recruiting function starts to absorb the load. A CEO who isn't allocating that much time to networking with future hires isn't doing the job, in his framing, regardless of how busy day-to-day operations feel.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Marc Barros, Co-founder Moment (interviewed 2015-10-06)

To keep a growing team comfortable with the constant churn of trial hires (people cycling through short paid side projects before a full offer), Moment deliberately maintains a rotating cast of outside collaborators at all times — Barros calls it a "collection of bandits" — so that new faces showing up to work on projects becomes normal rather than threatening to existing staff. They reinforce this by requiring documentation (e.g., sprint cards) to be written clearly enough that an outsider could understand it, which both supports the rotating-collaborator model and forces better internal communication.

Barros calls out a common first-time-founder hiring mistake: getting anchored on job titles (should this be a VP or a director? someone with experience or without?) instead of first defining the actual outcome you need help with. His fix, learned the hard way at Contour, was to strip titles away entirely at Moment and instead ask "what are we trying to accomplish, and who can help with that" — deliberately trying roles like PR in-house first so you learn what the function is actually worth before you go hire or contract for it.

Parker relays advice from his first CFO's father that reframes a common temptation: encountering an amazing candidate you have no current role for. ### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Dave Parker, CEO CodeFellows (interviewed 2017-01-12)

LiquidPlanner restructured its employee referral bonus so payout scales with the number of people an employee refers, rather than paying a flat one-time bonus per hire — the more people an employee refers, the more money that employee makes. Pearce says she prefers this over leaning primarily on paid recruiters because it's 'better for the ecosystem': it turns hiring into employees vouching for people they'd actually want to work alongside, while still functioning as a real financial incentive rather than a token gesture.

### [Type C -- color/anecdote (instructive context, not directly actionable)] Jason Stoffer, Partner Maveron (interviewed 2015-12-07)

Stoffer illustrates how brutal the senior-talent hiring market had become by describing a specific case from his own portfolio: a company trying to hire someone they considered a junior developer found that candidate had also received a job offer from Google for $320,000 a year. The number is used as concrete evidence for his broader claim that hiring senior (or even junior-level, in a hot market) talent had become a 'knife fight,' since even non-senior candidates were commanding compensation that would once have been reserved for experienced engineers.

## Product & MVP Strategy

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Enrique Godreau III, Partner 9Mile Labs (interviewed 2014-06-27)

Godreau describes how a fault-tolerant software company (self-rebooting systems with zero downtime) survived after its core telecom market died in the dot-com bust: instead of dying with the market, they identified two other industries -- defense and media -- that had the exact same underlying technical requirement (high system availability) even though the customers and sales motions were completely different. They chose to push into defense despite brutal, multi-year sales cycles because they had just enough residual telecom revenue to survive the wait; once established in defense (eventually running on 100% of U.S. fighter jets, three-time Lockheed software company of the year), the same technical core let them expand into media (video streaming), and shortly after unifying all three markets on one technical foundation and open-sourcing the product, the company was acquired. The reusable idea is to pivot by finding a different industry that needs the same underlying technical capability, not by abandoning your technology when your first market disappears.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Marc Barros, Co-founder Moment (interviewed 2015-10-06)

At Contour, Barros and his team spent significant engineering resources adding GPS and wireless connectivity features to their action camera while neglecting the core lens quality, shipping a camera with a subpar lens. The lesson he draws is that early-stage hardware teams have a strong pull toward feature accumulation ("what's next") instead of nailing the single core function competitors will judge them on first. GoPro, by contrast, focused obsessively on making a great waterproof action camera and won on that basic execution.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Marc Barros, Co-founder Moment (interviewed 2015-10-06)

Barros argues that the products that raise huge amounts on Kickstarter are usually technically the hardest to execute and are disproportionately launched by first-time hardware founders — which is exactly the profile most likely to fail to deliver, feeding Kickstarter's reputation for big promises and missed shipments. He says the actually sound approach is the opposite: keep the first product simple, ship it, make money, then use that to fund a slightly more ambitious second product, and repeat — but this approach is harder to fund because investors are drawn to big, ambitious pitches even though they have no ability to judge how hard the product actually is to deliver.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dan Lewis, CEO Convoy (interviewed 2016-08-10)

Lewis uses an insider account of Amazon's reviews team to illustrate a specific organizational failure mode: the team was originally formed to solve the problem of giving online shoppers confidence to buy without being able to touch or ask about a product, and their solution was customer reviews. But once the team was literally named "Reviews," every subsequent hire and goal became about making reviews go up and to the right - more reviews, more countries, more platforms - and the team stopped revisiting the original problem. It took a deliberate return to the underlying problem (customer confidence) to produce later features like customer Q&A and fit information ("does this run big or small"), which Lewis cites as a concrete case of a solution-named team missing adjacent opportunities.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dan Lewis, CEO Convoy (interviewed 2016-08-10)

Convoy initially assumed truck drivers could be handled like Uber drivers and built its app for Android only, reasoning that most drivers had Android phones and offering to loan an Android device to anyone with an iPhone until an iOS app existed. The hidden flaw was a difference in usage pattern: an Uber driver has their phone in hand constantly because every job is real-time, whereas a trucker is executing today's job while getting notified about tomorrow's - so truckers kept using their own existing phone for calls with dispatchers and wouldn't check Convoy's separate device until the end of the day, since the information wasn't time-sensitive. Recognizing this forced Convoy to build an iOS app quickly to recover, and Lewis frames it as a case where an assumption borrowed from an adjacent, superficially similar business (real-time ride-hailing) didn't transfer to a non-real-time logistics workflow.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dan Lewis, CEO Convoy (interviewed 2016-08-10)

In hindsight, Lewis believes Convoy over-built its initial MVP by fully automating both sides of its two-sided marketplace (an app for truckers/carriers and a web experience for shippers) rather than restricting the initial engineering investment to a single side. His retrospective advice, informed by a machine-learning background, is that founders should pick the one or two highest-priority steps in a workflow to build with real technology up front, and run everything else manually behind the scenes - deliberately using manual, non-scalable processes to validate the scenario before committing engineering and management bandwidth to automating the rest.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dave Parker, CEO CodeFellows (interviewed 2017-01-12)

Parker argues founders systematically conflate their long-term vision of the business ('at scale') with what the company actually is on day one ('at launch'), and that this confusion is one of the two most harmful founder mistakes. He illustrates with Uber: at launch it was purely a two-sided marketplace matching riders and drivers; only at scale did it add transaction-fee capture and other business-model layers, the same way Airbnb only added subscription and transaction-fee elements after establishing its core marketplace. The takeaway is that a startup gets to execute exactly one business model at launch — the future-state vision with multiple revenue streams doesn't exist yet and pursuing it prematurely burns runway before the core model is proven.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Joe Heitzeberg, CEO Crowd Cow (interviewed 2016-11-13)

Joe's MVP philosophy inverts the usual 'build something quick and rough' advice: the goal of an MVP test is specifically to make the experience look and feel like a fully real, finished product — a real price, a real checkout, a real confirmation email saying 'you're on the hook' — even though almost nothing behind it is built out yet. The point is to force a genuine buying commitment (credit card charged, share claimed) rather than settling for soft interest, because soft interest from an unfinished-looking page under-tests real demand.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Joe Heitzeberg, CEO Crowd Cow (interviewed 2016-11-13)

After running a hardware Kickstarter project (Poppy 3D), Joe concluded that hardware startups are structurally much harder to bootstrap than software ones — not because of manufacturing cost, but because a viable connected-hardware product simultaneously requires embedded software, cloud software, mobile software, industrial design, mechanical engineering, and electrical engineering, and no single founder or small founding team realistically has all of those skills. Software startups, by contrast, can reach meaningful scale with just the skills already sitting on a two- or three-person founding team, which is why he steered away from hardware for his next venture.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Howard Behar, Former President Starbucks (interviewed 2018-05-10)

In Starbucks's early years the stores had almost no seating -- just four or five bar stools in the window -- because the company saw itself as a transactional coffee-bean retailer that wanted customers 'in and out,' not lingering. Behar says the leadership team went 'kicking and screaming' into adding real chairs, resisting the change even as customers kept asking for it, because it meant altering the business model they'd built. The shift was triggered partly by the book The Great Good Place (about the value of a 'third place' between home and work) and by observing a University District coffee bar, the Allegro, that already had seating and regulars who sat and talked. Once Starbucks added chairs, the stores stopped being just a coffee-and-bean retailer and became a community gathering spot -- the 'third place' positioning that later defined the brand -- and Behar is explicit that this change, not the beverage business alone, is what turned Starbucks into a place people returned to daily.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Glenn Kelman, CEO Redfin (interviewed 2015-09-02)

Kelman is skeptical of how far "minimum viable product" thinking has been taken in startup culture -- he says it can become an excuse to ship something the team isn't actually proud of, deferring real quality indefinitely into "the next iteration." His counter-position: a team should be willing to say a product isn't good enough to ship yet even under pressure to move fast, because an MVP mindset taken too far erodes standards rather than just accelerating shipping.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Liz Pearce, CEO LiquidPlanner (interviewed 2014-06-27)

Pearce contrasts LiquidPlanner's product philosophy with traditional tools like Microsoft Project: conventional software requires a human to input the schedule and then manually re-propagate every change when priorities shift, which breaks down in practice because a human has to remember to make that update and often doesn't. LiquidPlanner's alternative has users rank work by priority instead of assigning dates, and the software calculates projected completion — on the belief that software can predict completion timing more reliably than a person can under constantly shifting priorities. The transferable idea: if a planning system depends on a human remembering to re-enter changed information, the plan will silently go stale — the fix is having the system infer state from a lower-maintenance input (like priority order) instead of demanding the human maintain the schedule directly.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Rand Fishkin, CEO SparkToro (interviewed 2018-09-02)

Fishkin directly rejects the widely repeated startup maxim "if you're not embarrassed by your first version, you launched too late," calling it a dirty lie for founders who, like him at SparkToro, already have a large, attentive audience. He points out that Eric Ries's original Lean Startup argument for MVPs assumed a company would be lucky to get five people to look at its first release — not tens of thousands of marketers who will form a permanent first impression on launch day. His conclusion is that MVP advice is being misapplied by influential venture voices to founders for whom a botched first impression is much more costly than the standard advice accounts for.

## Market Timing & Competitive Dynamics

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Enrique Godreau III, Partner 9Mile Labs (interviewed 2014-06-27)

Godreau backed a company that negotiated deals with taxi providers to let feature-phone users summon a cab with special carrier codes -- essentially Uber's concept -- 13 years before Uber existed. The founder later texted him the press release of Uber's $400 million raise with the message 'rats, 13 years too early.' Godreau's takeaway is that being early is functionally just as fatal as being late, but it's a much easier mistake to make because early conviction feels like foresight rather than a timing error; the market has to be ready to buy, not just able to be sold to.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dan Levitan, CEO Maveron (interviewed 2014-06-27)

Levitan cites concrete market-sizing numbers behind Maveron's Trupanion (pet health insurance) investment: Americans spend an estimated $51 billion per year on pets, roughly 25% of pets in the UK are insured, versus only about 2% in the US at the time — a gap of over 10x between a comparable developed market and the US. This kind of stat is a template for how Maveron sizes opportunity: not just "is this a big market" in the abstract, but finding a specific cross-market penetration gap that implies a large amount of unrealized demand.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dave Parker, CEO CodeFellows (interviewed 2017-01-12)

Parker tells a story about a friend (Voyager Capital's founder) who funded an Uber-like, text-message-based ride service roughly eight years before Uber succeeded, sinking a substantial sum of money into it — and the idea still failed. When Uber hit a billion-dollar valuation, the founder of that earlier failed company called to claim vindication ('I told you I was right'), and the response was that he'd been right about the idea but catastrophically wrong about timing. Parker's point is that being early is the single most expensive way to be wrong in a startup, because a visionary founder convinces themselves the market is just about to catch up — right up until the runway runs out.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Glenn Kelman, CEO Redfin (interviewed 2015-09-02)

When Redfin had built the best real-estate map technology, the easier and more typical path (the one many similar startups take) would have been to stay a media/lead-gen business -- attract consumer traffic and sell leads to traditional agents. Kelman instead chose the much harder path of becoming the brokerage itself, hiring real estate agents market by market, knowing it would take roughly a decade to scale nationally and require far more capital than a pure software play. He made this call specifically because handing a disrupted, tech-savvy consumer off to an unchanged traditional agent at the point of sale felt like a betrayal of the mission.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Glenn Kelman, CEO Redfin (interviewed 2015-09-02)

Kelman points out a counterintuitive feature of the real estate agent market: even though consumers often feel commission fees are too high, individual agents aren't actually earning much, because there are far more licensed agents in the U.S. than there is customer demand to support them, so most agents only close a handful of deals a year. The implication: the industry's problem isn't simply "agents overcharging" -- it's a structurally inefficient market where high aggregate fees coexist with low per-agent earnings, which is why he expects internet-driven demand generation, rather than fee-cutting alone, to reshape the business.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Rand Fishkin, CEO SparkToro (interviewed 2018-09-02)

Fishkin attributes Moz losing its position as the leading SEO software company to SEMrush and Ahrefs specifically to a strategic decision to broaden beyond core SEO rather than double down on it. The company diversified into a wider marketing category at exactly the moment competitors stayed narrowly focused on SEO tooling and out-executed Moz on its own turf. It's a concrete, named-competitor admission of a specific strategic misstep rather than a vague statement about losing focus.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Joe Heitzeberg, CEO Crowd Cow (interviewed 2016-11-13)

As part of top-down market sizing for Crowd Cow, Joe cites the total US retail beef market at roughly $100 billion, with the grass-fed beef segment (Crowd Cow's specific niche) growing about 40% year over year — numbers he uses to argue the category is large and expanding enough to support a venture-scale outcome, even though the company started by literally selling a single cow to friends.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Glenn Kelman, CEO Redfin (interviewed 2015-09-02)

As concrete evidence (not just a vibe) that tech workers were starting to leave the Bay Area for cheaper secondary markets, Kelman cites Redfin's own internal data: about 13% of Redfin's Portland home-search business in 2015 came from users who started their search while based in the Bay Area, up from roughly 3-4% just a year earlier. He treats that jump as an early signal that Silicon Valley's talent and wealth concentration was starting to disperse into cities like Seattle, Austin, and Denver.

## Pricing & Unit Economics

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Marc Barros, Co-founder Moment (interviewed 2015-10-06)

Barros breaks down why selling hardware through big-box retail (e.g., Best Buy, Apple Stores) is a cash trap that isn't obvious until you're in it: retailers pay on long terms (net 90) while you must pay your suppliers much sooner (net 60), creating a multi-month negative cash float. Banks won't bridge that gap because they typically only lend against 50% of inventory or receivables value — so a $100 sale might only get you $50 of financing against it, well short of what's owed. In the Apple ecosystem specifically he cites a 60% margin structure across roughly 450 stores, which sounds attractive until the payment-timing math is run.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dave Parker, CEO CodeFellows (interviewed 2017-01-12)

Parker gives a hard numeric gate for deciding whether to quit a day job: the ratio of customer lifetime value (LTV) to customer acquisition cost (CAC) must exceed 5-to-1. Below that ratio, he says explicitly not to leave the day job, regardless of how good the idea feels emotionally. This turns a fuzzy 'is this business viable' question into a single calculable number founders can check against their own funnel data before making an irreversible life decision.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dave Parker, CEO CodeFellows (interviewed 2017-01-12)

As a simplified pricing heuristic, Parker suggests splitting a product's price into thirds: roughly a third covers the cost of building/delivering the product, a third covers the cost of acquiring the customer (CAC), and a third is the actual profit margin. Using his example of a $100 product, that's roughly $33 for cost of goods, $33 for CAC, and $34 profit. The practical value is giving founders a fast sanity check on whether a proposed price point can actually sustain a business, rather than pricing based on gut feel or what feels fair to charge early on.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Dave Parker, CEO CodeFellows (interviewed 2017-01-12)

Parker pushes back against applying the consumer-product freemium playbook to B2B products. In consumer apps, a de-featured free tier can work fine because the core experience still has value. In B2B, he argues there's usually no way to strip out features without gutting the product to the point that the free version is worthless and doesn't actually demonstrate the product's value — so the freemium model, common wisdom for growth in consumer software, doesn't transfer to B2B the way founders often assume it will.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Julie Sandler, Partner Madrona Venture Group (interviewed 2014-10-23)

Sandler names the specific metrics she says actually matter when evaluating an early-stage company, as distinct from the many that don't. The core one is quantifiable customer reaction — capturing the funnel from an email address, to a free version, to a paid version — paired with understanding cost per acquired customer against lifetime value (LTV), which she says is preferred over lifetime revenue because it accounts for gross margin. Alongside that, she says payback period (how many months until an acquired customer's value covers what it cost to acquire them) is a number worth building reporting for from day one, not something to bolt on later once investors ask for it.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Joe Heitzeberg, CEO Crowd Cow (interviewed 2016-11-13)

Joe deliberately skips writing a traditional business plan, viewing it only as a communication tool that forces structured thinking with outside advisors — not as a planning tool he actually relies on. Instead, he tracks the business almost entirely through live spreadsheets, and notably, has real-time unit economics (net margin per individual order) coded directly into Crowd Cow's own admin software from a very early stage, so he can see the profitability of a given order as it comes in rather than reconstructing it later from a spreadsheet.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Jonathan Sposato, Chairman GeekWire (interviewed 2014-06-27)

Picnik was self-funded ('self-angel') by its three co-founders with no outside VC or angel money. At roughly 12 employees and about $60,000/month in payroll, each of the three founders personally wrote a $20,000 check every month to cover it — a state that persisted for 18 months to two years before the company reliably generated more revenue than expenses. He names this sustained personal cash exposure, not the earlier idea-stage struggles, as the second-biggest challenge of the company, and notes that visible founder sacrifice (later extending to pay cuts during the 2008 financial crisis) built real trust with early employees who understood the founders were absorbing more risk than they were.

## Ops & Failure Modes

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Enrique Godreau III, Partner 9Mile Labs (interviewed 2014-06-27)

Early in his career, Godreau had a portfolio company that beat its sales forecast by 10-12% quarter over quarter for two straight years -- until it suddenly missed two quarters in a row and never recovered. The root cause, uncovered only when he asked to see usage data, was that the sales team had been re-selling/renewing existing customers ('icing' deals) who had stopped actually using the product; bookings looked healthy while adoption was dead, and the company simply ran out of a finite pool of people willing to buy without using. The lesson he draws is that revenue growth alone is a lagging and misleading signal -- usage data is the real leading indicator of whether a market is durable.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Joe Heitzeberg, CEO Crowd Cow (interviewed 2016-11-13)

As Crowd Cow's order volume ramped up, they were silently losing one order per week somewhere in the warehouse for three to four weeks in a row before the team stepped back and treated it as a structural signal rather than a one-off glitch. Their response wasn't just a tactical fix — it was a deliberate reallocation of time and focus away from pure growth and toward operational infrastructure, on the reasoning that growth built on top of a broken fulfillment process would only compound the problem.

### [Type A -- live gap (names a specific unsolved pain point or market gap)] Liz Pearce, CEO LiquidPlanner (interviewed 2014-06-27)

Pearce argues startups have the opposite data problem from big companies: instead of analysis paralysis from too much data, early-stage companies typically aren't instrumented enough to produce the data they'd need, and don't have the luxury of spending two weeks to go get it. Her resolution is to look 'forensically' at whatever fragmentary data exists — even a single data point — and pair it with gut judgment to decide fast, rather than waiting for a fuller picture that won't arrive in time. This reframes 'data-driven decisions' at an early-stage company as necessarily incomplete by design, not a discipline gap to close with more dashboards.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Joe Wallin, Partner Davis Wright Tremaine LLP (interviewed 2014-06-27)

Wallin describes a specific institutional bias he's observed in how U.S. immigration authorities treat startup founders on H-1B visas: officials are on guard against "paper companies" set up purely to manufacture a basis for immigration status, and startups get caught in that suspicion by default because they structurally look like shell companies -- no office space, little cash in the bank, few employees -- even when they are legitimate, fast-growing businesses. This means a real startup founder on an H-1B faces extra scrutiny not because of anything they did wrong, but simply because the early-stage characteristics that are normal for any startup (thin balance sheet, no office, small headcount) happen to match the profile immigration officials are trained to flag.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Howard Behar, Former President Starbucks (interviewed 2018-05-10)

Early in his tenure, Behar pushed to raise Starbucks store wages to a dollar above minimum wage, projecting the cost impact would be a modest roughly one percentage point hit to the P&L. When the actual numbers came in, the real cost was double what he'd projected -- about two percentage points, not one -- a meaningful miscalculation for a thin-margin retail business scaling fast. He frames this not as a minor rounding error but as a genuine misjudgment of cost, and ties it back to a broader self-assessment that his biggest career failure wasn't any single number but the lack of self-belief that made these kinds of decisions harder than they needed to be.

### [Type B -- replicable practice (a tactic/heuristic another founder could copy)] Adrian Hanauer, Owner Seattle Sounders FC (interviewed 2016-04-26)

The Sounders' sports-science program runs on a specific, named stack: a heart-rate monitor and a GPS unit worn by every player at every training session, tracking acceleration, deceleration, distance, and speed, synchronized with drill data, plus a system called Omega Wave that measures central-nervous-system function to gauge sleep and recovery — about eight systems in total, feeding a database that a former Microsoft data scientist turns into usable graphs for coaches. As a concrete example, Hanauer recalls that during a 5v5 small-sided training game, Clint Dempsey's heart rate hit 97% of his max early in the drill, a red flag the staff could act on regarding his conditioning. The specificity of which vendors are used, how the data streams are combined, and who processes it is the kind of operational detail only someone actually running the program would know.
